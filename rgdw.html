Abstract

Self-taught engineers building production AI systems face a structural asymmetry: their engineering skill outpaces their academic grounding. When a PhD reviewer asks "why this approach?", a blog post and a weekend of intuition do not survive scrutiny. The standard response — promise to do more research next time — fails because discipline does not scale and because the engineer remains the bottleneck in their own quality loop.

This paper presents the Research-Grounded Development Workflow (RGDW): a structural system in which a fleet of specialized agents classifies every non-trivial engineering decision, dispatches parallel research sprints, adversarially attacks its own recommendations, fact-checks load-bearing claims, and enforces quality gates before any output reaches the human operator. Evidence quality comes from architecture, not from willpower. The operator reviews conclusions, never the process.

The system is grounded in a 93-job-description corpus showing research capability is table-stakes for AI agent engineering roles, a published methodology for evaluating production agents (companion paper), and empirical findings from multi-agent research pipelines at industry labs. Its core contribution is not any single technique but the combination of classifier–enforcer separation, pre-registered disconfirmation, red-team agents instantiated separately from the validators they attack, and auto-revision loops that resolve high-confidence attacks without human intervention. The result is an evidence trail that withstands expert scrutiny, produced automatically, on every decision that matters.

The self-taught gap: why discipline is not enough

Engineering skill and academic grounding are separable. An engineer can ship a working system without ever reading the paper that explains why their approach works — or fails. This asymmetry is invisible until the moment someone qualified asks why. At that moment, an evidence trail that does not exist cannot be summoned.

A grounded-theory analysis of 93 job descriptions across 12 AI engineering organizations — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, Microsoft, Scale, Sierra, Cognition, Cursor, Databricks, Perplexity, Hugging Face — shows research capability is not an optional senior-level differentiator. It is explicitly required in 18 of the top 20 roles under terms like "stay current on AI/ML research and tools," "research and implement state-of-the-art using foundation models," and "research cutting-edge algorithms for integration into the training stack." Cross-functional communication roles add the converse requirement: defending technical choices in design reviews, customer conversations, and field-feedback reports back to Research and Product.

The standard response to this gap is intention: I will do more research next time. Discipline-based solutions fail in practice for three reasons.

First, discipline does not scale with decision volume. A single engineer shipping a production agent makes dozens of T2-and-above decisions per week — framework choice, validation library, auth pattern, caching strategy, orchestration topology. Manual research at the required depth for even a third of these saturates the engineer's calendar and leaves no time to actually build.

Second, the engineer becomes the bottleneck in their own quality loop. A human-in-the-middle workflow — engineer researches, engineer judges, engineer decides — is the exact pattern that collapses under load. Decisions get made faster than research is done; the gap between stated process and actual process widens; the evidence trail quietly empties out.

Third, a single engineer cannot be their own red team. Confirmation bias is not a discipline problem — it is a cognitive default. Validating a recommendation and attacking it require different postures, and the same person holding both postures sequentially produces worse attacks than two separate parties.

The RGDW treats these three failure modes as structural, not motivational. It solves them by delegating research, challenge, fact-checking, and gate enforcement to separate agents — each operating under constraints that prevent the cognitive collapse a solo human cannot avoid.

Prior work: how others approach engineering decisions

Existing approaches to evidence-backed engineering decisions fall into four clusters. Each contributes a necessary component but none combine to produce an enforceable pipeline.

1 · Architectural decision records (ADRs)

ADRs — popularized by Nygard (2011) and refined across engineering org guidance from Google, Spotify, and ThoughtWorks — document the what and why of a decision at the moment it is made. They are narrative artifacts. Their strength is traceability: six months later, the team can reconstruct the reasoning. Their limitation is procedural: ADRs do not require research to have happened, do not classify source quality, and do not challenge the recommendation. An ADR citing a single vendor blog post is, structurally, as valid as one citing a meta-analysis.

2 · DORA research and documentation-driven engineering

The DORA program of research establishes that documentation quality correlates with delivery performance, and that written design docs are a signature of high-performing organizations. This validates having evidence trails but says nothing about how those evidence trails should be produced. It answers "does writing it down matter?" (yes) but not "what counts as sufficient evidence?"

3 · Academic systematic review methodology

PRISMA (Page et al., 2021), grounded theory (Strauss & Corbin, 1998), and the broader systematic-review tradition in evidence-based medicine and social science prescribe rigorous research protocols: pre-registration of search strings, source classification, contradiction resolution, explicit exclusion criteria. These methods directly inspire RGDW's source-tier schema and pre-registered disconfirmation mechanics. Their limitation for engineering work is time-cost: a PRISMA-compliant systematic review takes weeks per question, which is incompatible with a shipping cadence measured in hours.

4 · Multi-agent research pipelines at industry labs

Published practitioner work on LLM-based research systems (Anthropic's multi-agent research architecture, 2025; published agent evaluation methodologies from industry labs) establishes that orchestrator-worker patterns with parallel subagent dispatch outperform single-agent research on complex queries, that "detailed task descriptions prevent duplication" across subagents, and that end-state evaluation is more tractable than per-step evaluation for stateful agents. Anthropic's reported finding — a multi-agent research system outperforming a single-agent baseline by 90.2% on internal evals, at 15× the token cost — is one of the more quantitatively clear signals that parallel decomposition matters for research quality.

These works provide architecture patterns but not engineering-decision discipline: they describe research pipelines for answering user questions, not pipelines that gate-check their own outputs before an engineer commits code. The missing layer is adversarial proofing hooked directly into the development workflow.

The gap this paper addresses
No prior system combines: (1) automated research triggered by decision classification, (2) source-tier enforcement at the schema level, (3) adversarial red-team agents structurally separated from validators, (4) auto-revision loops that resolve high-confidence attacks, (5) fact-checking before delivery, and (6) BLOCK-level quality gates enforced by an agent other than the one that produced the recommendation. The RGDW does.

The RGDW architecture

The RGDW is an eight-stage pipeline executed by six specialized agents — architect, conductor, scout, deep-diver, synthesizer, eval-engineer — each with a bounded role and no permission to act outside it. The architecture's core property is structural separation between recommender and enforcer: no single agent can both produce a recommendation and approve its own gate.

3.1 · Decision classification

Every engineering decision is classified into one of three tiers by the architect agent. The classification determines whether research is required, how much time-box the research sprint gets, and which quality gates apply. The conductor agent — a different instance — enforces the classification by dispatching the corresponding pipeline. An architect cannot downgrade its own classification without conductor approval with documented rationale.

3.4 · Adversarial proofing pipeline

The adversarial proofing layer is the core differentiator. Every T2+ recommendation is attacked before it is delivered. The attack is not a stylistic check — it is a structurally separate agent tasked with destroying the recommendation using pre-registered disconfirming evidence it cannot know in advance.

Stage 1 · Pre-registered disconfirming search strings

Before scouting begins, the architect commits 3–5 search strings specifically designed to find evidence against each option. These strings are logged before results arrive and cannot be modified afterward. Scouts must execute them and report results, even when empty. This prevents post-hoc disconfirmation theater — the pattern where researchers invent weaker attack queries once they know what the winning option is.

"PydanticAI limitations" OR "PydanticAI problems" OR "PydanticAI production issues"
"PydanticAI vs" AND ("regret" OR "switched away" OR "migration")
"LangGraph complexity" OR "LangGraph debugging nightmare"
"agent framework" AND ("regret" OR "switched from" OR "abandoned")

Stage 2 · Red-team agent attack

A separate deep-diver instance — never the one that validated the supporting evidence — receives only the recommendation and its charter is to destroy it. The red-team agent searches for counterevidence, identifies failure conditions, proposes the strongest counterargument, and rates attack confidence HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW.

Stage 3 · Auto-revision loop

The critical architectural commitment: high-confidence attacks automatically revise the recommendation without human intervention.

The 3-cycle cap prevents infinite loops and makes unresolved contests legible: a recommendation still contested after 3 revisions is a genuine uncertainty, not a workflow failure. The operator sees either a survived recommendation or a clearly-framed contested decision — never a raw battle-log to arbitrate manually.

Stage 4 · Fact-check

Before delivery, the eval-engineer in verification mode spot-checks 3–5 load-bearing claims from the ER package: URLs resolve, quoted numbers match the actual source, tiers are classified correctly. If fabrication is detected — a hallucinated citation, a misquoted statistic, a misattributed source — the pipeline BLOCKS, removes the bad ER, and recycles. This is the fabrication firewall. Nothing false reaches the operator even when the upstream agents hallucinate.

3.6 · Knowledge base

Every completed decision persists to a Knowledge Base as a structured Evidence Record package. Subsequent decisions in the same domain check the KB before launching fresh research sprints. The KB is not a passive archive — it is staleness-aware, event-driven, and shared across projects.

Staleness model

Time-based staleness sets baseline expiration by evidence type — pricing claims expire at 90 days, API/SDK behavior at 180 days, architecture patterns at 365 days, peer-reviewed findings at 730 days. Event-driven invalidation supersedes time-based: a dependency major-version bump invalidates ERs citing that dependency; a published CVE invalidates security-critical claims immediately; a vendor acquisition invalidates vendor-claim ERs; and the operator's production experience contradicting an ER triggers manual re-research with the tag contradicted_by_experience.

KB reuse protocol

Before any research sprint: (1) check KB for existing ER on the topic; (2) if present and status: active and within staleness window, cite and proceed without re-research; (3) if present but stale, run a lighter refresh sprint that checks whether key claims still hold; (4) if absent, run the full research sprint. Cross-project reuse is first-class: an ER produced for Project A is valid for Project B as long as the claims still hold in B's context.

Structural integrity: why the system cannot be gamed

The RGDW's claim to produce defensible evidence rests on structural properties, not on agent discipline. Seven commitments make the pipeline resistant to the failure modes that collapse discipline-based research protocols.

  1. Classifier and enforcer are different agents. The architect classifies; the conductor enforces. An agent that creates a BLOCK cannot clear it by reclassifying the decision as trivial — downgrades require explicit conductor approval with documented rationale.
  2. Red-team agent is a separate instance. The deep-diver that validates supporting evidence does not also attack it. A fresh instance receives only the recommendation. This prevents the cognitive trap where the validator's confirmation bias inherits into the attacker.
  3. Pre-registered search strings are committed before scouting. The architect locks in disconfirming queries before results arrive, making post-hoc theater — "we looked for counterevidence and happened not to find any" — detectable. Scouts must report results even when empty.
  4. Fact-check is a fabrication firewall. A separate eval-engineer instance verifies URLs, numbers, and source tiers before delivery. Even when an upstream agent hallucinates a citation, the firewall catches it. BLOCK on detection, recycle, deliver clean.
  5. Auto-revision changes recommendations, not just annotates them. HIGH-confidence attacks produce new recommendations, not warning labels on the old one. The operator does not arbitrate — the pipeline has already done so.
  6. Source tier enforcement is schema-level. An ER cannot exist without a tier. A T3 decision cannot pass Gate 2 without ≥2 Tier 1/2 sources. Weight is not optional honest behavior — it is a structural constraint in the data model.
  7. Event-driven invalidation prevents KB rot. Dependency bumps, CVEs, vendor pivots, and contradicting production experience all invalidate ERs without waiting for calendar-based staleness. The KB cannot silently decay into stale authority.

The composite effect: an adversary — or a lazy version of the engineer — cannot force a weak recommendation through the pipeline by holding any single role. Every gate has a different enforcer, every recommendation has an attacker, and every load-bearing claim has a fact-checker.

Integration with the engineering workflow

The RGDW does not sit alongside the engineering workflow — it sits inside it. When the operator assigns a task, the conductor routes it and the architect decomposes it into decisions. T1 decisions proceed immediately with the agent's training knowledge. T2 and T3 decisions trigger the automated research pipeline, which runs in parallel with other work. The operator receives battle-tested recommendations asynchronously and reviews only the final output.

Walkthrough: a T3 orchestration-framework decision

The following walkthrough traces a real T3 decision — selecting an agent orchestration framework for a production data-extraction pipeline — through the full RGDW. It is presented to make the abstract pipeline concrete.

Phase 0 · Classification (5 min)

The architect classifies the decision T3: it affects system topology, introduces new recurring infrastructure cost, and touches a domain the operator has limited production experience in. Pre-registered disconfirming searches are committed before scouting begins.

Phase 1 · Scouting (90 min, 3 scouts in parallel)

Scout A sweeps academic sources — arXiv papers on agent orchestration, published practitioner guidance on effective agent patterns. Scout B sweeps practitioner sources — GitHub Issues, migration reports, HN threads above 50 points, developer benchmarks. Scout C sweeps vendor documentation and official repositories, capturing contributor counts, release cadence, open-issue ratios.

Phase 2 · Validation (60 min)

The deep-diver runs Generate-Critique-Revise on each surviving signal. Published industry-lab guidance that "the most successful implementations start with the simplest possible solution" is captured as Tier 2 evidence supporting the raw-SDK option and opposing the heaviest framework. Conflict of interest is noted — the lab has incentive to recommend approaches using its SDK — and documented in the ER.

Phase 3 · Synthesis (30 min)

The synthesizer discovers a scope conflict: one cluster of evidence shows Framework X excels at structured I/O, another shows Framework Y excels at stateful graph workflows. Resolution: this is a scope conflict, not an evidence conflict. Under the current pipeline's needs — data extraction, simple chains — Framework X fits. Under future branching-workflow needs, Framework Y would fit. Outcome type: recommended.

Phase 4 · Red team (30 min)

A separate deep-diver instance receives only the recommendation. It finds three attack vectors: the smaller community around Framework X, practitioner debugging reports, no mature observability tool comparable to what Framework Y users rely on. Attack confidence: MEDIUM.

Phase 5 · Auto-revision (15 min)

MEDIUM confidence: no forced revision. The synthesizer incorporates the observability gap as a known risk in the recommendation, attached with the mitigation that OTEL instrumentation can close the gap with engineering effort.

Phase 6 · Fact-check (15 min)

The eval-engineer spot-checks four claims: published-guidance URL resolves (PASS), Framework X star count matches at time of check (PASS), cited CVE reference verified (PASS), benchmark number matches the source document (PASS). No fabrication detected.

Phase 7 · Gate check (10 min)

Gate 2: PASS (7 ERs, Tier 1/2 sources per option, pre-registered searches executed). Gate 3: PASS (red team delivered at MEDIUM, incorporated as known risks; fact-check clean). Delivery authorized.

Phase 8 · Final package delivered to operator

Recommendation: Framework X for Phase 1.
Why: Structured I/O focus matches current needs. Evidence: 7 ERs across 5 independent sources.
Known risks: Observability gap (requires OTEL instrumentation). Smaller community. Migration path to Framework Y untested.
Red team verdict: MEDIUM — recommendation survived. Observability gap is real but addressable.
Revisit when: workflow needs branching within 90 days, or Framework X maintainer activity stalls.
Full evidence trail: persisted to KB at the decision's canonical ID.

Total automated pipeline time: ~4 hours, run in parallel with other engineering work. Operator review time: 5–10 minutes. Evidence trail: citation-ready, defensible under expert scrutiny, persisted across projects.

Implementation: wiring RGDW into a live agent fleet

The RGDW specification was integrated into six live agent specs on 5 April 2026. Each spec received a bounded addition — a new section covering its RGDW responsibility — without modification to existing content. All six were then graded against the Hervey 2026 15-dimension framework. The RGDW additions improved every spec's score with zero regressions. Three of six entered the session below the B minimum (203/270) from pre-existing structural gaps; all three were brought to B minimum before the implementation was accepted.

7.2 · The improvement loop

Architect required five revision passes before clearing the B threshold; builder required three. The binding constraint in both was identical: no self-eval infrastructure. Neither spec had defined eval fixtures, trace schemas, or pass^k targets — the dimensions that distinguish a spec a fleet can improve from one that can only fail silently.

The improvement pattern confirmed a structural observation: D-grade specs cannot be fixed in one pass. The eval framework's dimension weights mean that high-leverage gaps (D6b Eval Infrastructure, weight 9%) require specific additions — fixture sets, CI runners with assertion logic, dataset sourcing protocols — that are meaningless if written at too-high a level of generality. Targeted additions per dimension, graded after each pass, outperformed front-loaded comprehensive rewrites on every iteration.

Eval-engineer presented a different failure mode: its raw score crossed B (205.1/270) after one revision pass, but a D15-RI C-cap held it at C grade. The cap triggers when fewer than four of eight D15-RI research-integrity criteria score ≥3, regardless of raw score. Eval-engineer's Fact Check Mode lacked disconfirmation documentation and falsifiability enforcement — the two criteria that make a verification report reproducible rather than opaque. Adding them lifted the cap and the grade in a single targeted pass.

What the implementation revealed about the fleet
The three D/C-grade specs were not failing because of RGDW. They were failing because the fleet had no standard for eval infrastructure. The implementation exposed a pre-existing fleet-wide gap: most agents lacked the self-measurement capability required to know whether they were degrading. The RGDW additions provided the trigger to address this — but the underlying problem predated the workflow.

7.3 · ETZ-001: the first live decision

An integration test dispatched the architect with a real T2 decision: should the ETZ compliance pipeline use Temporal Cloud or Prefect for workflow orchestration? The architect classified the decision T2 rather than T3. The rationale: prior domain research exists in the ETZRESEARCH knowledge base. The KB reuse protocol triggered on the first attempt — the operator entered an existing domain, not a new one, and the pipeline handled the distinction correctly without instruction.

The synthesizer produced an ER package from five sources across practitioner and vendor-official tiers. The deciding factor was the durability model: Temporal replays exact workflow state via event sourcing; Prefect caches completed task results and re-runs from task boundaries. Limitations and future work

The RGDW is a production workflow, not a proven methodology. Six honest limitations bound its current claims.

Compute cost and time-cost

A T3 decision consumes ~4.5 hours of agent compute and an order of magnitude more tokens than an unaided decision. Published multi-agent research pipelines at industry labs report comparable overhead: roughly 15× the token usage of single-agent chat for equivalent queries, offset by a 90%+ improvement on research-eval tasks. Whether that tradeoff is worth it depends on the decision's irreversibility. For T3 decisions affecting system topology or security, yes. For T1 decisions, it would be indefensibly wasteful — which is why the classifier exists.

Source-tier ceiling

Most real engineering decisions lack Tier 1 evidence. Peer-reviewed studies comparing production frameworks in deployed contexts do not exist for most framework choices. Practitioner tier evidence — corroborated migration reports, benchmarks with disclosed methodology — partially fills the gap, but it is structurally weaker. A decision cited 90% from practitioner sources is more fragile than one grounded in peer-reviewed benchmarks. The schema surfaces this weakness; it cannot eliminate it.

LLM research agents have their own biases

Recall biases in search-indexed content, recency biases in training data, SEO-optimized farm content crowding out authoritative sources — these are documented failure modes of LLM-based research. Published post-mortems from multi-agent research pipelines note that early agents "consistently chose SEO-optimized content farms over authoritative but less highly-ranked sources like academic PDFs." RGDW's source-tier schema and fact-check firewall mitigate but do not eliminate this class of error.

The system accelerates expertise, not replaces it

Domain expertise comes from reviewing decisions, not from avoiding them. The auto-generated decision summaries persisted to the KB, and the domain-expertise tracker that gates the T3→T2 transition, are designed to compound learning — but the operator still has to read the summaries. A decision stack accumulated without review is not expertise; it is a queue of unread mail.

No formal evaluation of decision-quality improvement

The strongest claim the current work can make is qualitative: decisions produced through the RGDW carry evidence trails that survive expert scrutiny, and decisions made without the RGDW frequently do not. A before/after comparison with blinded evaluators rating decision quality — similar to the psychometric validation applied in the companion evaluation-framework paper — is open future work.

Dependency on specific infrastructure

The RGDW as implemented depends on a subagent orchestration environment with parallel dispatch, shared blackboard, and fact-check tooling. Porting it to other orchestration runtimes is possible but non-trivial, and the walkthrough's specific agent names and handoff conventions would not transfer directly. The methodology is portable; the specific implementation is not.

Future work

  1. Blind-evaluator study comparing RGDW-produced decisions to unaided decisions on defensibility, traceability, and correctness under domain-expert review
  2. Cross-project KB reuse telemetry — measuring how often prior ERs short-circuit fresh research, and the decay rate of reuse across time
  3. Red-team attack calibration — validating that HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW confidence ratings predict downstream decision reversals
  4. Integration with eval frameworks from the companion paper so that production agent failures automatically invalidate the ERs that recommended the failing architecture

Conclusion

The self-taught gap is real, but it is not a discipline problem. Attempts to solve it with better intentions collapse under decision volume and confirmation bias. The RGDW's wager is that research quality comes from structure, not discipline — that adversarial agents, pre-registered searches, separate enforcers, and BLOCK-level gates produce better evidence trails than willpower ever could.

The deeper insight is this: evidence trails are not just for defense. Bad ideas die in red team before they become code. A recommendation that has already survived a HIGH-confidence attack is a better recommendation, not just a more-documented one. The pipeline's output is not paperwork — it is architecturally-improved decisions.

The agent fleet operating under RGDW constraints is the methodology in action. Every classification, every gate, every fact-check is executed by an agent with a bounded role and no permission to bypass. The operator reviews conclusions; the structure does the rest.

For self-taught engineers building production AI systems, the question is no longer "will I have time to do the research?" It is "does my workflow make research a bottleneck or a background process?" The RGDW answers the second question structurally — and, in doing so, closes the gap between what a self-taught engineer can build and what they can defend.
