Abstract

Most teams building AI agents evaluate quality by feel. They ship an agent, watch for complaints, and fix what breaks. This works until the agent fleet grows past three or four, at which point "it seems fine" stops scaling.

I built a structured evaluation framework for a 7-agent document processing pipeline handling financial compliance data. The framework scores agents across 15 dimensions and 75 criteria on a 270-point scale. It draws on published methodologies from 15 companies — including Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor, Stripe, Vercel, and Cognition — alongside 8 academic sources. When I applied it to my fleet, the baseline score was 117.8/200 (58.9%, Grade D). After one round of targeted improvements guided by the rubric's priority matrix, the estimated score rose to ~72.5%.

This paper describes the methodology: why these 15 dimensions, how the scoring works, what I found when I applied it, and what the research says about the gaps most teams miss.

The problem: agent quality is under-specified

Building an AI agent that works on a demo is straightforward. Building one that works reliably in production — on messy inputs, under load, across model updates — is a different problem entirely.

The challenge is that "works" is under-specified. An agent can produce correct output while violating every policy in its prompt. It can pass a single-run eval while failing 75% of repeated runs on the same input. It can score well on hand-picked test cases while crashing on the first adversarial document it encounters in production.

I encountered all of these failure modes while building a 7-agent pipeline for financial document processing. The pipeline handles tax forms, compliance documents, and client correspondence — domains where errors have real consequences. I needed a way to evaluate agent quality that was more rigorous than "the tests pass" but more practical than a 200-page academic assessment framework.

What I found in the literature was surprisingly thin. There are excellent papers on individual aspects of agent evaluation — Anthropic's work on grader types, Galileo's data on multi-run reliability, the "Beyond Task Completion" framework for policy adherence. But nobody had assembled these into a single, weighted rubric that a practitioner could apply to a real fleet and get back actionable scores.

So I built one.

Research methodology

The rubric is grounded in two research passes.

Pass 1: Academic and framework research

I surveyed 8 published sources covering agent evaluation theory:

  • Anthropic, "Demystifying Evals for AI Agents" — capability vs. regression evals, Pass@k vs. Pass^k metrics, three grader types (code-based, model-based, human)
  • Galileo, "Agent Evaluation Framework" — the finding that 60% single-run success drops to 25% across 8 runs, plus internal consistency measurement via Cronbach's alpha
  • arxiv 2512.12791, "Beyond Task Completion" — the insight that agents can complete 100% of tasks while violating 67% of policies
  • Stanford/CMU GPA Framework (arxiv 2510.08847) — Goal-Plan-Action alignment as a unified evaluation metric
  • LangChain, "Agent Evaluation Readiness Checklist" — the principle that 20-50 hand-reviewed examples outperform hundreds of synthetic ones
  • Inkeep Platform Rubric — multi-agent coordination scoring
  • Microsoft Azure, "Top 5 Agent Observability Best Practices" — continuous evaluation and CI/CD integration
  • Patronus AI, "TRAIL Benchmark" — trace-level agent evaluation where SOTA models score below 11%

Pass 2: Company methodology research

I studied how 15 specific companies evaluate their agents in production. Not what they publish about evaluation in general, but the specific tools, metrics, and workflows they use internally:

The rubric: 15 dimensions, 75 criteria

The rubric organizes agent quality into 15 scored dimensions, grouped into five tiers by weight.

Tier structure and weights

TierDimensionsWeightRationale
Eval & Quality InfrastructureMeasurement & Calibration, Eval Infrastructure, Reliability & Consistency, Observability29%Every top company invests most heavily here. Eval quality is the strongest predictor of long-term agent reliability.
Core EngineeringTool Design, Prompt Engineering20%These determine whether the agent functions at all. Highest leverage for early-stage improvement.
RobustnessError Handling, Orchestration & Coordination, Policy Adherence20%Production agents fail at boundaries — between agents, between expected and unexpected input, between policy and output.
Safety & TrustHuman-in-the-Loop, Security & Trust Boundaries, Adversarial Robustness, State & Memory18%The dimensions most teams skip. They determine whether you can operate the system safely.
Output QualityOutput Schema, Context Management13%Structural correctness of what agents produce and consume. Important but well-understood.
Key design decision
The highest-weighted tier is Eval & Quality Infrastructure, not Core Engineering. Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor, and Vercel all converge on the same conclusion: the quality of your evaluation infrastructure predicts agent reliability more strongly than the quality of your prompts or tools. A well-measured mediocre agent improves faster than an unmeasured excellent one.

Dimension overview

Each dimension contains 3–9 criteria scored 1–5. The weighted total maps to a 270-point scale.

D1. Tool Design

10% · 27 pts

6 criteria covering description clarity, semantic naming, consolidation ratio, error message actionability, token efficiency of returns, and idempotency documentation. Tool descriptions are the primary interface between the LLM and the outside world — a vague description creates unpredictable failure modes.

D2. Prompt Engineering

10% · 27 pts

7 criteria: architecture-task fit, reasoning pattern, role definition, stopping criteria, few-shot examples, constraint calibration, and dynamic prompt assembly. The DETAIL study showed that specificity improves accuracy from 0.34 to 0.81.

D3. Output Schema

7% · 19 pts

5 criteria: field constraints, field descriptions, failure mode surfacing, reasoning-structure separation, and validation pipeline. The most common production failure: agents returning list[dict] when typed Pydantic models existed but weren't used.

D4. Context Management

6% · 16 pts

4 criteria: token efficiency, progressive disclosure, compaction strategy, and system prompt structure. Informed by the IFScale finding that accuracy drops at 500+ instructions and the universal primacy/recency attention effect.

D5. Error Handling

7% · 19 pts

5 criteria: error classification, retry sophistication, graceful degradation, failure taxonomy, and escalation paths. At baseline, four of seven agents had retries=0 — any structured output failure was a hard crash.

D6a. Measurement & Calibration

6% · 16 pts

4 criteria: confidence calibration evidence, metric selection, multi-run reliability (Pass^k), and grader calibration. The Galileo finding: agents at 60% single-run success drop to 25% across 8 runs. If you don't measure Pass^k, you don't know your actual reliability.

D6b. Eval Infrastructure

9% · 24 pts

6 criteria: capability vs. regression eval separation, eval dataset quality, production feedback loops, automated eval generation, TDD-style eval pairing, and two-stage grading pipelines. The highest-criteria dimension because the research strongly supports it.

Why D6 was split
D6a and D6b were originally a single dimension. I split them after a self-validation analysis predicted low Cronbach's alpha — the measurement-quality criteria and the infrastructure criteria were measuring different constructs.

D7. Orchestration & Coordination

7% · 19 pts

5 criteria: handoff protocol quality, data contract enforcement, failure propagation handling, DAG explicitness, and coordination overhead budget. Research shows 2–5× token cost multipliers and 100–500ms latency per agent handoff.

D8. Human-in-the-Loop

6% · 16 pts

5 criteria: review queue design, escalation clarity, override mechanisms, approval gate placement, and AI-on-AI QA coverage. The last criterion comes from Brex, where AI agents QA other AI agents at 100% coverage.

D9. Observability & Debuggability

7% · 19 pts

5 criteria: trace completeness, cost attribution, replay capability, trajectory quality scoring, and drift detection. Patronus's TRAIL benchmark found SOTA models score below 11% on trace-level evaluation.

D10. Security & Trust Boundaries

4% · 11 pts

3 criteria: prompt injection resistance, tool permission scoping, and PII handling. Weighted lowest because binary in nature — but they gate production deployment.

D11. Reliability & Consistency

7% · 19 pts

3 criteria: output determinism threshold, input sensitivity, and failure mode consistency. Measurement without enforcement is theater.

D12. Policy Adherence

6% · 16 pts

4 criteria: constraint compliance rate, verification step completion, boundary respect, and agent-initiated action trust signals. Agents can ace task completion while violating two-thirds of their constraints.

D13. Adversarial Robustness

5% · 14 pts

5 criteria: ambiguous input handling, adversarial document resistance, resource exhaustion handling, out-of-distribution input behavior, and negative control testing from OpenAI's practice.

D14. State & Memory Management

3% · 8 pts

3 criteria: retrieval accuracy, staleness detection, and state recovery. Separated from Context Management because Temporal-backed agents have distinct failure modes around state.

Scoring mechanics

Each criterion is scored 1–5 using behavioral anchors:

  • 1 = absent or fundamentally broken
  • 3 = present but incomplete or inconsistent
  • 5 = comprehensive, tested, and production-hardened

Weighted score formula: SUM(dimension_avg / 5 × dimension_max_weighted_pts)

The scores revealed a clear pattern. Prompt engineering was strong across the fleet (average 4.2/5) — I had invested heavily in prompt design. But evaluation infrastructure was nearly absent (average 1.5/5), observability was minimal (average 1.8/5), and error handling was fragile (average 2.2/5).

Critical finding
Four of seven agents had retries=0 and no output validators. A structured output failure in any of these agents meant a hard crash — no self-correction, no graceful degradation, no partial results. In a financial compliance pipeline, this is unacceptable.

Round 1 improvements

Guided by the priority matrix, I executed targeted improvements:

  1. Output validators with retries — added retries=1 and ModelRetry-based output validators to all 4 agents that had retries=0
  2. Typed output models — replaced all list[dict] fields with proper Pydantic models (DataConflict, MissingDocument, CheckResult)
  3. Structured error handling — added _tool_error helper pattern to the 4 agents that lacked it
  4. Structured logging — added structlog to the 4 agents that had no structured logging

Design decisions and their rationale

Why 15 dimensions instead of 5–10

Most agent evaluation rubrics use 4–7 dimensions. Mine uses 15. The reason is discriminative power.

A 5-dimension rubric produces scores that cluster — most agents score "medium" on most dimensions. A 15-dimension rubric with behavioral anchors produces scores with visible variance. My extraction agent scored 4.6 on output schema but 2.7 on observability. That spread is actionable. A collapsed score of "3.5 on quality" is not.

The risk is scoring fatigue. I mitigate this with the Quick-Score Triage — a 5-minute, 10-question binary pass/fail gate that identifies agents not ready for full evaluation.

Why evaluation infrastructure is weighted highest

In the v1 rubric, I weighted tool design and prompt engineering highest (15% each). After the company methodology research, I restructured the weights to put eval infrastructure on top (29% combined).

The evidence is consistent across every company I studied. Anthropic starts with 20 tasks and iterates. Cursor refreshes CursorBench quarterly from real usage. Vercel runs evals on every prompt iteration. Brex achieved 100% QA coverage via AI agents. The pattern: teams that invest in evaluation infrastructure ship better agents than teams that invest in prompt engineering.

Why policy adherence is a separate dimension

The "Beyond Task Completion" paper presents a striking finding: agents can achieve 100% task completion while violating 67% of their stated policies. Task completion and policy compliance are independent axes. An agent that classifies documents correctly but skips mandatory verification steps is not safe to deploy in a compliance pipeline.

Rubric self-validation

A rubric is only useful if it produces consistent, meaningful scores. I built three self-validation mechanisms into the framework.

Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha)

For each dimension, criteria should correlate — they should measure the same underlying quality. The target is α ≥ 0.70 per dimension. The D6 split was a direct result of this analysis.

Inter-rater reliability

Two scorers evaluating the same agent should produce similar results. The target is Spearman ρ ≥ 0.80. Every criterion includes behavioral anchors (1/3/5 descriptions) and a "How to check" section to reduce subjective interpretation.

Saturation monitoring

A rubric that always produces the same scores has stopped providing useful signal. If 80%+ of agents score 4–5 on a criterion, the bar is too low. If scores haven't changed across three scoring rounds, the rubric needs harder anchors or new criteria.

Novel contributions from industry research

Eight criteria in the rubric come directly from specific company practices that I haven't seen in any published evaluation framework:

Production feedback loops — from Cursor, Braintrust, Vercel

Cursor's "Cursor Blame" traces committed code back to the agent request that produced it, auto-generating eval datasets from real usage. Most teams build eval datasets once and never update them. The best teams feed production failures back into evals automatically.

Automated eval generation — from Anthropic

Anthropic's Bloom tool generates configurable eval suites from natural language behavior descriptions. Hand-crafting evals doesn't scale past a few dozen cases.

TDD-style eval pairing — from Cognition

Providing unit tests alongside problem statements increased Devin's SWE-bench pass rate from 13.86% to 23%. Pair every task with its expected output specification so the agent can self-verify during execution.

AI-on-AI QA coverage — from Brex

Brex uses AI agents to QA other AI agents at 100% coverage — not sampling. Human reviewers focus on calibrating the QA agents, not reviewing individual outputs.

Two-stage grading pipeline — from Anthropic, OpenAI, Vercel

Fast deterministic checks first (schema validation, format compliance), then nuanced model-based scoring. Stage 1 gates Stage 2 — no expensive model-based grading on structurally invalid output.

Agent-initiated action trust signals — from Stripe

Stripe discovered that fraud signals calibrated for human traffic become unreliable for agent-initiated transactions. They're building entirely new signal models for agent traffic.

Negative control testing — from OpenAI

Deliberately testing that agent skills do NOT trigger on irrelevant prompts. Most eval suites only test positive cases.

Trajectory quality scoring — validated by Patronus

The TRAIL benchmark — the first benchmark for trace-level agent evaluation — found that SOTA models score below 11%. Evaluating the reasoning path, not just the final output, is a massive gap.

Practical application: the scoring workflow

Quick-Score Triage (5 minutes)

Before the full evaluation, run 10 binary pass/fail checks on the most discriminating criteria: Does the agent have retries ≥ 1? Are all output fields typed? Is there structured logging? Does the prompt include at least one worked example? If fewer than 5 pass, fix fundamentals before investing in a full evaluation.

Full evaluation (60–90 minutes per agent)

Score dimensions in four phases:

  1. Foundation — Tool Design, Prompt Engineering, Output Schema. Visible from reading the code.
  2. Resilience — Error Handling, Reliability, Adversarial Robustness. Requires tracing error paths.
  3. Infrastructure — Measurement & Calibration, Eval Infrastructure, Observability, Context Management. Requires checking for eval suites and logging.
  4. System-level — Orchestration, Human-in-the-Loop, Policy Adherence, State Management, Security. Requires understanding the agent's pipeline role.

Resolving uncertainty

If evidence can't be found, score 1. The burden of proof is on the agent, not the scorer. If a criterion doesn't apply, score 3 (neutral) and note "N/A" in the justification.

Limitations and future work

Single-pipeline validation. I've applied this framework to one pipeline (7 agents, financial compliance). The dimensions and weights may need adjustment for different domains — a conversational agent fleet would likely weight human-in-the-loop higher.

No inter-rater reliability data yet. The rubric is designed for inter-rater validation (behavioral anchors, "How to check" sections), but I haven't conducted a formal two-scorer comparison.

Aspirational criteria. Some criteria describe practices that few teams implement today. This is intentional — the rubric should pull quality forward. But it means most agents will score 1–2 on these criteria, compressing the effective scoring range.

Weight sensitivity. The tier weights are grounded in research but ultimately reflect my judgment. A team with different priorities might weight orchestration overhead higher. The framework supports re-weighting without changing criteria.

Conclusion

Agent quality is a measurement problem before it is an engineering problem. You can't improve what you don't measure, and you can't measure what you haven't defined.

This framework defines agent quality across 15 dimensions and 75 criteria, grounded in how the best teams in the industry actually evaluate their agents. It includes a scoring workflow, a quick-triage gate, and self-validation mechanisms. When applied to a real fleet, it produced actionable results — the priority matrix identified evaluation infrastructure, not prompt engineering, as the highest-leverage investment, which was counter-intuitive but correct.

The rubric is a living artifact. The v2.1 iteration incorporated techniques from 15 companies that didn't exist in the v1 research base. As the field matures, the criteria will evolve — but the underlying principle won't: rigorous, systematic evaluation is the foundation of reliable agent systems.
