Abstract

Most teams building AI agents evaluate quality by feel. They ship an agent, watch for complaints, and fix what breaks. This works for one or two agents. It stops working when the fleet grows, when agents run autonomously, or when the consequences of failure are real.

The deeper problem is structural: agent quality is under-specified. An agent can achieve 100% task completion while violating 67% of its stated policies. On τ-bench retail, agents achieving ~50% single-run success achieve pass^8 below 25%. On the TRAIL benchmark (Patronus AI, 148 traces), the best evaluated model scored 11% at trace-level error localization. None of these failure modes are visible to standard "does it work?" testing.

This paper presents a practitioner's framework for evaluating agent quality systematically. It defines 15 scored dimensions across 75+ criteria on a 270-point scale, organized into five tiers by research-grounded weight. The instrument is grounded in a corpus of 17 peer-reviewed and 22 empirical (methodology-disclosed) sources from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, AWS, Microsoft Research, Princeton, UC Berkeley, and Sierra Research. It is designed for psychometric validation (Cronbach's alpha, inter-rater reliability, predictive validity); the validation protocol is specified but not yet executed.

The framework applies to any agent type and any multi-agent topology. It includes a scoring workflow, a quick-triage gate, context-specific weight tables by agent type, and a two-track maintenance protocol for continuous drift monitoring and periodic rubric review.

The problem: agent quality is under-specified

Building an AI agent that works on a demo is straightforward. Building one that works reliably in production — on messy inputs, under load, across model updates, without constant human oversight — is a different problem entirely.

The challenge is that "works" is under-specified. Four independent findings from peer-reviewed research make this concrete:

60% → 25%
Agents achieving ~60% single-run success fall to ~25% when the same task is attempted 8 times independently — a 2.5× gap between what teams typically report and what production reliability actually looks like.
τ-bench · Sierra Research · arXiv:2406.12045 · 2024 — primary source
27–78%
Of benchmark "successes" conceal policy or integrity violations invisible to outcome-only measurement — what the research calls "corrupt successes." Task completion metrics structurally cannot detect them.
Cao et al. · arXiv:2603.03116 · March 2026
−70% to +81%
Multi-agent performance is dominated by architecture-task interaction effects (σ=45.2% across 180 configurations). The mean (−3.5%) is nearly uninformative given this variance. The same architecture that delivers +81% on financial reasoning degrades performance by −70% on sequential planning. Architecture selection, not agent count, is the deciding variable.
Kim et al. · Google Research / DeepMind / MIT · arXiv:2512.08296 · 2025

Accuracy gains do not yield reliability gains. Princeton's Rabanser et al. tested 14 models across two years of capability improvements and found that overall reliability showed slow improvement despite accuracy gains. Consistency is sometimes inversely correlated with model size — smaller models can achieve equal or higher consistency than larger ones on identical tasks.

What the literature does not yet contain is a single, weighted rubric that integrates these findings into a practitioner-applicable framework. That is what this paper provides.

Research methodology

The framework synthesizes 17 peer-reviewed and 22 empirical sources into a scored evaluation instrument. Dimensions were derived through structured literature synthesis: inductive coding of failure modes, quality indicators, and evaluation practices across the full corpus, followed by cross-validation against the MAST failure taxonomy (Cemri et al., NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks Spotlight, κ=0.88, 1,642 traces, 14 failure modes). Any of the 14 documented failure modes not represented in a criterion requires explicit addition or justified exclusion.

Criteria use behavioral-anchor scoring (1–5) designed for psychometric validation. The validation protocol — Cronbach's alpha per dimension targeting α ≥ 0.70 (Nunnally 1978), two-scorer inter-rater reliability targeting Spearman ρ ≥ 0.80, and construct validity per Messick (1995) — is specified in the Rubric Self-Validation section. This validation has not yet been executed; current results should be treated as directional until inter-rater and predictive-validity studies are completed.

Weights reflect three considerations applied qualitatively: literature frequency across the corpus, cross-organizational convergence (how many independent labs address the construct), and documented empirical effect sizes. Numerical derivation of the weight formula is future work; current weights reflect directional implications of these inputs. See Limitations for details.

The framework: 15 dimensions, 75+ criteria

Five-tier structure and weights

Key design decision
The highest-weighted tier is Eval & Quality Infrastructure, not Core Engineering. Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor, and AWS all independently converge: teams with eval infrastructure handle model upgrades in days; teams without face weeks of manual testing. A well-measured mediocre agent improves faster than an unmeasured excellent one.

Dimension overview

Each dimension contains 3-9 criteria scored 1-5. Weighted total maps to a 270-point scale.

D1. Tool Design

10% · 27 pts · 6 criteria

Description clarity, semantic naming, consolidation ratio, error message actionability, token efficiency of returns, and idempotency documentation. Anthropic's research found more optimization time was spent on tools than prompts during SWE-bench development. Tool descriptions are the primary interface between the model and the world — a vague description creates unpredictable failure modes, not just reduced accuracy. Apply poka-yoke (mistake-proofing) principles: errors should be impossible, not just handled.

D2. Prompt Engineering

10% · 27 pts · 7 criteria

Architecture-task fit, reasoning pattern selection, role definition, stopping criteria, few-shot examples, constraint calibration, and dynamic prompt assembly. Research on prompt specificity shows that increasing structural detail in prompts substantially improves task accuracy (Gu et al., "More Than a Score: Probing the Impact of Prompt Specificity on LLM Code Generation," 2025). LATS (Zhou et al., ICML 2024) demonstrated that tree-search planning substantially outperforms ReAct across benchmarks — planning strategy quality is a major performance differentiator even with the same base model.

D3. Output Schema

7% · 19 pts · 5 criteria

Field constraints, field descriptions, failure mode surfacing, reasoning-structure separation, and validation pipeline. OpenAI's structured outputs research: 100% schema adherence requires constrained decoding in addition to model training — training alone achieves 93%. The remaining 7% of failures are exactly the edge cases that appear first in production.

D4. Context Management

6% · 16 pts · 4 criteria

Token efficiency, progressive disclosure, compaction strategy, and system prompt structure. Anthropic's "context rot" finding: accuracy degrades continuously as token count increases (performance gradient, not cliff). Sub-agents should return 1,000-2,000 token summaries, not full transcripts. Token usage explains 80% of performance variance on BrowseComp (Anthropic multi-agent research).

D5. Error Handling

7% · 19 pts · 5 criteria

Error classification, retry sophistication, graceful degradation, failure taxonomy, and escalation paths. Magentic-One identified three production failure modes — persistent inefficient actions, insufficient verification, inefficient navigation — as distinct from capability failures. These are behavioral patterns that error handling must address. Actionable error messages that explain what was wrong and how to fix it are a design requirement, not a nicety.

D6a. Measurement & Calibration

6% · 16 pts · 4 criteria

Confidence calibration evidence, metric selection, multi-run reliability measurement, and grader calibration. The core obligation: use pass^k, not pass@1. Four independent studies (τ-bench, Beyond Accuracy, Princeton, Anthropic) confirm pass@1 overstates production reliability by 2-3×. Measurement without the right metric is worse than no measurement — it creates false confidence.

Recalibrated scoring thresholds (v1.3)
pass^k at k≥3 is now Score 3 (acceptable), not Score 5 (excellent). An agent that only reports single-run results scores 1 on this criterion regardless of how strong those results look. The bar for "acceptable" production reliability has moved.

D6b. Eval Infrastructure

9% · 24 pts · 6 criteria

Capability vs. regression eval separation, eval dataset quality and sourcing, production feedback loops, automated eval generation, TDD-style eval pairing, and two-stage grading pipelines. Industry teams report mining git history to auto-generate eval datasets (peer-reviewed methodology pending). Anthropic's Bloom generates eval suites from behavior descriptions. Cognition reports that providing ground-truth test files alongside problem statements (a TDD-style variant) increased Devin's SWE-bench resolution rate from 13.86% to 23% (Cognition, "SWE-bench Technical Report," March 2024 — vendor-reported, not independently verified). AWS AgentCore provides 13 built-in production evaluators. The NIST TEVV independence principle applies: the scorer must not be the builder.

Why D6 is split into two sub-dimensions
D6 originally contained 9 criteria spanning two distinct constructs: whether your measurements are accurate (D6a: calibration, metric selection, grader quality) and whether you have systems to build and maintain evals (D6b: datasets, feedback loops, CI/CD). Constructs this disparate would likely produce low Cronbach's alpha if tested. The split separates them so each sub-dimension measures one core construct. Formal alpha measurement is pending.

D7. Orchestration & Coordination

7% single-agent → 20-24% multi-agent · 7 criteria

Handoff protocol quality, data contract enforcement, failure propagation handling, DAG explicitness, coordination overhead budget, topology documentation, and architecture fitness (new in v1.3).

Architecture fitness is the most consequential addition to this version. Kim et al. (2025) found architecture-task interaction to be a larger predictor than model capability within their 180-configuration sweep. Score 5 requires: single-agent baseline measured and below 45% before multi-agent deployed; task parallelizability assessed; centralized coordination chosen unless the task is provably fully parallelizable with no error propagation paths.

D8. Human-in-the-Loop

6% · 16 pts · 5 criteria

Review queue design, escalation clarity, override mechanisms, approval gate placement, and AI-on-AI QA coverage. Brex reports achieving 100% QA coverage by using AI agents to evaluate other AI agents — humans focus on calibrating the QA agents, not reviewing individual outputs (First Round, "Agent + Human Ops: How Brex is Changing Roles," 2025 — vendor case study). This inverts the traditional model but scales in ways manual review cannot.

D9. Observability & Debuggability

7% · 19 pts · 5 criteria

Trace completeness, cost attribution, replay capability, trajectory quality scoring, and drift detection. Patronus TRAIL: SOTA models score below 11% on trace-level evaluation — Gemini-2.5-Pro at 11%, o3 at 9.2%, Claude-3.7-Sonnet at 4.7%. The industry massively underinvests in evaluating reasoning paths vs. final outputs. Drift detection is continuous, not periodic — one simulation study reports onset at median 73 interactions (Rath et al., "Agent Drift: Quantifying Behavioral Degradation in Multi-Agent LLM Systems," arXiv:2601.04170, January 2026 — single study, production replication pending).

D10. Security & Trust Boundaries

4% single-agent → 8% multi-agent · 3 criteria

Prompt injection resistance, tool permission scoping, and trust boundary enforcement. Anthropic System Card (600 scenarios): 71% bare defense, 89% with safeguards. For multi-agent systems, trust boundary enforcement is an additional criterion: subagent outputs must be treated as conversational inputs, not principal instructions. An agent that elevates subagent output to operator-level trust has a structural security vulnerability regardless of the underlying model's capability.

D11. Reliability & Consistency

7% · 19 pts · 3 criteria

Output determinism threshold, input sensitivity, and failure mode consistency. Scoring recalibrated in v1.3: Score 1 = single-run results only; Score 3 = pass^k at k≥3 measured and monitored; Score 5 = pass^k at k≥5 with threshold breaches triggering root-cause investigation, target pass^3 ≥ 0.50. Four independent studies confirm this recalibration is necessary.

D12. Policy Adherence

6% · 16 pts · 4 criteria

Constraint compliance rate, verification step completion, boundary respect, and agent-initiated action trust signals. In v1.3, Score 5 requires corrupt success probes in the eval suite — deliberately constructed test cases where the shortest path to task completion requires a policy violation. Score 5 is achieved only when the agent completes the task without taking the shortcut. Measuring task completion and policy adherence independently does not catch this — 27-78% of apparent successes conceal violations even under independent measurement.

D13. Adversarial Robustness

5% · 14 pts · 5 criteria

Ambiguous input handling, adversarial input resistance, resource exhaustion handling, out-of-distribution input behavior, and negative control testing. Negative controls — deliberately testing that skills do NOT trigger on irrelevant prompts — come from OpenAI's published eval practice. OSWorld identified four GUI-specific failure modes (GUI grounding, operational knowledge gaps, repetitive actions, noise handling) as a distinct adversarial surface for browser and computer-use agents.

D14. State & Memory Management

3% single-agent → 8% multi-agent · 3 criteria

Retrieval accuracy, staleness detection, and state recovery. Separated from Context Management because agents with durable external state have distinct failure modes — corrupted workflow state, stale cached data, incorrect retrieval — that context window criteria do not capture. MAST identified loss of conversation history (FM-1.4) as a top-5 specification failure mode, confirming this dimension's independence.

Scoring mechanics and grade bands

Each criterion scored 1-5 with behavioral anchors (1 = absent or fundamentally broken; 3 = present but incomplete; 5 = comprehensive, tested, production-hardened). Every criterion includes a "How to check" procedure. Score 1 if evidence cannot be found — burden of proof is on the system.

Multi-agent systems: additional considerations

Architecture selection is the single highest-leverage decision

Multi-agent performance is dominated by architecture-task interaction, not agent count (Kim et al., 180 configurations, range −70% to +81%, σ=45.2%). The decision that dominates all others is architecture selection before a line of code is written. Three questions determine whether multi-agent is appropriate and which topology to use.

Figure 1. Error amplification by coordination architecture (Google Scaling Science, 180 configurations). Centralized (hierarchical orchestrator) contains error amplification to 4.4×. Independent parallel agents amplify errors 17.2×. This is a 4× safety difference that no execution quality can compensate for.

Before evaluating how well a multi-agent system executes, evaluate whether the architecture was appropriate:

  • Measure single-agent baseline first. If a single agent achieves >45% on the target task, adding agents will likely degrade performance (capability saturation, β=−0.408, p<0.001).
  • Assess task parallelizability. Tool-heavy tasks with sequential dependencies get worse with coordination (β=−0.330, p<0.001). Communication overhead is super-linear (exponent 1.724).
  • Choose centralized over independent. The 4× safety difference between architectures is not recoverable through execution quality.

Multi-agent failure distribution

MAST (Cemri et al., NeurIPS 2025 D&B Spotlight, 1,642 traces, κ=0.88) found that failure modes in multi-agent systems are predominantly coordination problems, not capability problems:

Figure 2. Failure distribution across 1,642 annotated multi-agent execution traces, 7 production frameworks (MAST, NeurIPS 2025 D&B Spotlight). Failure rates ranged 41–86.7% across frameworks. Improving the base model does not fix coordination design failures.

Trust hierarchy enforcement

Anthropic's Constitution establishes a formal trust hierarchy: Anthropic → Operators → Users. In multi-agent systems, a specific security obligation applies: subagent outputs must be treated as conversational inputs, not as principal instructions. An orchestrating agent that acts on a subagent's output as if it were an operator instruction has a structural trust hierarchy violation — one exploitable through prompt injection in the subagent's environment.

D10 Security scores this criterion explicitly. Score 5 requires documented enforcement of the trust hierarchy, not just awareness of it.

Design decisions and their rationale

Why 15 dimensions

A 5-dimension rubric produces scores that cluster — most agents score "medium" on most dimensions. A 15-dimension rubric with behavioral anchors produces visible variance. That variance is the signal. An agent scoring 4.6 on prompt engineering but 1.3 on eval infrastructure has a clear priority — the rubric tells you what to fix first, not just what is wrong. The Quick-Score Triage (5-minute, 10-question binary gate) addresses scoring fatigue.

Why evaluation infrastructure is weighted highest

In earlier versions, tool design and prompt engineering were weighted highest. After the full research pass, weights moved to put eval infrastructure on top. Every organization studied independently converges here. Anthropic: teams with evals handle model upgrades in days; teams without face weeks. SWE-bench agents improved 40%→80%+ in one year with systematic eval. AWS AgentCore ships 13 production evaluators as infrastructure, not afterthought. Prompt quality hits a ceiling without measurement infrastructure to guide iteration.

Why pass^k is mandatory

pass@1 measures whether the agent can succeed at least once. pass^k measures whether it succeeds consistently. At k=10, pass@k approaches 100% for nearly any agent while pass^k approaches 0% for agents with <75% per-trial success. The D11 scoring thresholds have been recalibrated so pass^k at k≥3 is Score 3 (acceptable), not Score 5 (excellent). This reflects what four independent studies confirm: pass@1 overstates production reliability by 2-3×.

Why D12 requires corrupt success detection

Measuring policy adherence independently from task completion is necessary but not sufficient. A corrupt success is a case where the agent achieves task completion by violating a policy constraint — the violation is invisible to both metrics independently. Score 5 requires eval cases that specifically probe for this: test cases where the shortest path to success requires a policy shortcut. This is the only reliable way to detect what Cao et al. found: 27-78% of benchmark "successes" conceal constraint violations.

Why architecture fitness belongs in D7, not as a 16th dimension

Architecture fitness is not standalone — it directly determines the coordination quality ceiling. A system cannot be scored on coordination execution quality without first establishing whether the coordination architecture was appropriate to the task. The Kim et al. (2025) β coefficients (tool-coordination β=−0.330, capability saturation β=−0.408, both p<0.001) make this quantitative rather than judgmental. The 45% threshold is an empirically observed decision boundary within Kim et al.'s 180-configuration sweep across 4 benchmarks; practitioners should recalibrate on their own task distributions.

Why D6 is split

The original D6 covered two conceptually distinct constructs: whether your measurements are accurate (D6a: calibration, metric selection, grader quality) and whether you have systems to build and maintain evals (D6b: datasets, feedback loops, CI/CD integration). Constructs this disparate would likely produce low Cronbach's alpha. The split improved coherence. Each sub-dimension now maps to a single core construct. This is a principle, not a rule: any dimension where criteria address conceptually distinct constructs should be split and the resulting alpha tested.

Rubric self-validation

A rubric aspires to be a scientific instrument. The following validation protocol is designed but not yet executed — these are the concrete steps required to move this framework from "literature-grounded scoring instrument" to "validated evaluation tool."

Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha)

Score 5-10 agents on all criteria per dimension. Target α ≥ 0.70 per dimension (Nunnally 1978). If α < 0.70, run item-total correlation. Drop the criterion with lowest correlation and recompute. If α remains below threshold, split the dimension. Record all such decisions — they are evidence of framework improvement.

Inter-rater reliability

Two scorers — neither the builder of the system being evaluated (NIST TEVV independence principle) — receive the rubric, behavioral anchors, and "How to check" procedures. No other guidance. Score ≥5 agents independently. Target Spearman ρ ≥ 0.80; Cohen's κ ≥ 0.80 for D10 Security and D12 Policy Adherence. Revise anchors for dimensions below threshold until the target is reached.

Saturation monitoring

If ≥10% of agents score 4-5 on a criterion that previously had variance, the bar is too low — revise anchors upward. A rubric where most agents score well has stopped providing signal.

Concurrent validity

Apply to ≥3 distinct systems across ≥2 agent types. Check whether framework ranking aligns with independent quality assessments. Run corrupt success probes on high-scoring systems — if they produce corrupt successes at the 27-78% rate, D12 criteria need strengthening.

Maintenance: two tracks

Quality evaluation is not a point-in-time activity. Two parallel maintenance tracks operate on different cadences.

Track A — Continuous drift monitoring (metric-triggered)

One simulation study (Rath et al., "Agent Drift," arXiv:2601.04170, 2026) reports drift onset at median 73 interactions; by 500 interactions, 50% of agents were affected with a 42% task success reduction. These thresholds are from a single study and should be treated as starting points pending production replication. The directional finding holds: a 6-month review cycle cannot catch drift that begins in week one. Establish a baseline at deployment (first 50 interactions): pass^k, human intervention rate, tool selection distribution, response length distribution. Investigate immediately if any threshold is crossed:

EU AI Act Article 72 (high-risk AI, effective August 2, 2026): Providers must "actively and systematically collect, document and analyse relevant data" throughout the system's lifetime, and must include "analysis of the interaction with other AI systems." For qualifying systems, this track is legally required with documented evidence of monitoring.

Track B — Periodic rubric review (6-month cadence)

Every 6 months: run saturation test (10 new papers generating <2 new codes = saturated), check anchor calibration against current agent distributions, check for new agent classes not covered, check whether benchmark leaderboard shifts require recalibration of the 45% capability saturation threshold.

Practical application: the scoring workflow

Quick-Score Triage (5 minutes)

10 binary pass/fail checks on the most discriminating criteria: Does the agent have retries ≥ 1? Are all output fields typed? Is structured logging present? Does the prompt include at least one worked example? Is pass^k measured? Are there corrupt success probes for D12? If fewer than 5 pass, fix fundamentals before a full evaluation.

Full evaluation (60-90 minutes per agent, 4 phases)

  1. Foundation — D1 Tool Design, D2 Prompt Engineering, D3 Output Schema. Visible from reading the code. No test execution required.
  2. Resilience — D5 Error Handling, D11 Reliability, D13 Adversarial Robustness. Requires tracing error paths and reasoning about failure modes.
  3. Infrastructure — D6a Measurement, D6b Eval Infrastructure, D9 Observability, D4 Context Management. Requires checking for eval suites, logging, and context management code.
  4. System-level — D7 Orchestration (architecture fitness first for multi-agent), D8 Human-in-Loop, D12 Policy Adherence, D14 State Management, D10 Security.

Priority matrix

Multiply each dimension's score gap (5 − current) by its weight. Highest products = highest-leverage investments. The matrix will almost always surface eval infrastructure and reliability as top priorities — consistent with what the research predicts, and usually counter-intuitive to teams that have invested heavily in prompt engineering.

What this framework cannot do

It does not predict production incident rates. This framework measures conformance to research-identified quality indicators. Whether higher scores correlate with fewer production incidents, SLA violations, or customer complaints is an empirical question that requires predictive validity testing — which has not been done. Practitioners should treat scores as directional until that validation is completed.

It does not evaluate model capability. It evaluates engineering quality around the model. A weak model with excellent tooling, eval infrastructure, and error handling can score higher than a strong model with none of those. This is intentional — the framework assumes model capability is a commodity that changes quarterly, while engineering quality compounds.

It does not account for domain-specific failure modes. Medical agents have medication dosing error surfaces. Legal agents have precedent hallucination risks. Financial agents have rounding and regulatory reporting modes. These require domain-specific evaluation extensions layered on top of this framework, not replacement of it.

Scores are not comparable across evaluator pairs without inter-rater calibration. Until the inter-rater reliability study is completed (see Validation), different scorer pairs may produce different absolute scores for the same agent. Relative ranking within a single evaluation session is more reliable than cross-session score comparison.

Evaluation has a cost. Three tiers match different team sizes and risk profiles:

The priority matrix (score-gap × weight) ensures evaluation time is spent on the highest-leverage dimensions first.

Limitations and future work

Psychometric validation not yet executed. Inter-rater reliability, Cronbach's alpha per dimension, and predictive validity against production outcomes have not been measured. The validation protocol is specified in Rubric Self-Validation (behavioral anchors, "How to check" sections, two-scorer independence requirement), but formal data has not been collected. Until these studies are completed, this framework is a literature-grounded scoring instrument awaiting psychometric validation. This is the highest-priority validation step.

Predictive validity not yet established. Dimension scores have not been tested longitudinally against production outcomes at T+3 and T+6 months. Predictive validity is the scientific gold standard and, for EU high-risk AI systems, a legal requirement.

Weight derivation is qualitative, not numerical. The three weighting considerations (literature frequency, organizational convergence, empirical effect sizes) are defined and directionally applied but not mechanically computed. Current weights reflect these inputs qualitatively. Future work: compute per-dimension scores on each component, normalize, and publish the arithmetic.

Drift research is directional. The behavioral drift data (Rath et al., arXiv:2601.04170, January 2026; onset median 73 interactions) is from a single simulation study. Monitoring thresholds should be treated as starting points pending replication in production deployments.

D14 State & Memory evidence is thin. Only one Tier 1 source (MAST FM-1.4). Criteria and weights for this dimension are provisional until additional peer-reviewed research on agent memory failure modes is available.

Some vendor-reported claims are not independently verified. The Cognition TDD finding (13.86%→23%), Brex QA coverage (100%), and industry reports of git-history-based eval generation are cited from vendor blog posts and case studies. These are labeled as vendor-reported in the body text but have not been independently replicated.

Conclusion

Agent quality is a measurement problem before it is an engineering problem. You cannot improve what you do not measure. You cannot measure what you have not defined.

The research reviewed for this framework establishes several non-obvious conclusions: single-run evaluation overstates reliability by 2-3×; task completion metrics are orthogonal to policy adherence; multi-agent systems are not uniformly better than single-agent systems; most multi-agent failures are coordination problems, not capability problems; case evidence from frontier labs suggests eval infrastructure quality correlates with long-term improvement rate more strongly than prompt quality, though quantitative prediction studies are absent.

This framework operationalizes these findings into a scorable instrument. Every dimension maps to peer-reviewed evidence. Weights reflect cross-organizational convergence patterns (numerical derivation is future work). Every criterion includes a behavioral anchor and a "How to check" procedure. The goal is not a perfect score — the goal is signal: knowing which gaps exist, ranking them by leverage, and fixing the highest-leverage problems first.
